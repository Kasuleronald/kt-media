Tips & Guides

Buying Guide

5 Things to Check Before Buying a Business Laptop in Kampala

Read the full guide

1. RAM matters more than the sticker price. An 8GB machine handles browsers, spreadsheets and video calls comfortably. Anything heavier — design work, many tabs, accounting software — needs 16GB.

2. SSD, not HDD. A solid-state drive boots and loads programs several times faster than an older hard disk, even on a budget machine.

3. Battery health on refurbished units. Always ask for the battery condition to be tested and disclosed before you buy a pre-owned laptop — it's the part that wears fastest.

4. Power adapter compatibility. Confirm you're getting the original or a matching charger; the wrong adapter can damage a laptop's battery circuit over time.

5. Local warranty and support. A cheaper machine with no local support can cost more in the long run. Buy from a seller who can also repair what they sell.

Maintenance

Why Is My Laptop Running Slow? 7 Common Causes

Read the full guide

1. Too many programs starting automatically with Windows. 2. A nearly-full hard drive — keep at least 15% free space. 3. Dust buildup causing the fan to overheat and throttle performance. 4. An aging battery forcing the CPU into power-saving mode. 5. Malware or unwanted browser extensions running in the background. 6. Outdated drivers or operating system. 7. A failing hard drive — if the laptop also freezes or makes clicking sounds, get it checked urgently.

Most of these are fixable in under an hour with a clean-up and a fan service. Bring your laptop in for a free diagnosis before assuming you need a new one.

Protection

Protecting Your Computer From Power Surges in Uganda

Read the full guide

Sudden power cuts and the surge that often follows when power returns are one of the most common causes of desktop and printer damage we see in our workshop. A UPS (uninterruptible power supply) does two jobs: it filters out surges, and it gives you a few minutes of battery runtime to save your work and shut down safely.

For a single office desktop, a basic UPS is usually enough. For a small server, router, or multiple machines, size the UPS to your total load — we can help you calculate this in-store. Surge-protected power strips are a cheaper minimum option, but they don't provide backup runtime like a UPS does.
